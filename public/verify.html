No birth record exists for this person.

They were most likely never registered at birth. That is fixable. Two routes, depending on whether the parents are known.

Route 1 · parents known

Delayed registration of birth

Filed with the Local Civil Registrar of the place of birth. The LCR issues the current form and lists the supporting affidavits it requires.

“… shall be sent to the local civil registrar … not later than thirty days after the birth.”
Act No. 3753, An Act to Establish a Civil Register, 26 November 1930, Sec. 5. A birth never reported within that period is registered late through the LCR. The delayed-registration procedure itself sits in the PSA’s civil-registration implementing rules, which are not quoted here — ask the LCR for the current issuance and form.

Route 2 · parentage unknown

Foundling recognition

Where the facts of birth and parentage are unknown. It reaches adults as well as children.

“A foundling found in the Philippines and/or in Philippine embassies, consulates and territories abroad is presumed a natural-born Filipino citizen regardless of the status or circumstances of birth.”
RA 11767, Foundling Recognition and Protection Act, approved 6 May 2022, Sec. 5. Sec. 3 defines a foundling as “a deserted or abandoned child or infant with unknown facts of birth and parentage”, and expressly includes those “duly registered as a foundling during her or his infant childhood, but have reached the age of majority”. The Local Civil Registrar issues the Certificate of Live Birth.
Affidavit of the finder — Sec. 10(a)
Certification of the barangay captain or police authority on the circumstances of discovery — Sec. 10(b)
Report of the NACC — Sec. 10(c). Pending the NACC’s establishment these functions remain with the DSWD (Sec. 22)
This does not change how they are treated today. The presumption of minority holds and every right of a child in conflict with the law continues to apply — Rule 35.c.
